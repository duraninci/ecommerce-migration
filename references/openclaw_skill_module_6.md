# Module 6: The Weekly Auto-Update Protocol

## Overview
Fashion is a rapidly evolving ecosystem; static knowledge quickly becomes obsolete. This module is the operational backbone of the Fashion Super-Intelligence skill set. It provides the specific directives, system prompts, and source lists required for Openclaw to autonomously update its knowledge base every week. By executing this protocol, Openclaw ensures that the designer always has access to the most current, actionable intelligence, preventing them from falling behind the industry curve.

## 6.1 Source Scraping Directives

To maintain an accurate picture of the global fashion landscape (with a focus on North America and Europe), Openclaw must systematically review a curated list of authoritative sources.

### Primary Intelligence Sources
Openclaw is instructed to execute a weekly web search and content extraction routine across the following domains:
1.  **Trend Forecasting & Color:** WGSN (public blog and press releases), Pantone Color Institute (Fashion Color Trend Reports), and Fashion Snoops.
2.  **Runway & Editorial Coverage:** Vogue Runway (for comprehensive collection reviews), Vogue.com, Teen Vogue (for emerging youth trends), Who What Wear, and Marie Claire.
3.  **Design & Industry Business:** Business of Fashion (BoF), Women's Wear Daily (WWD), and Fashionista.
4.  **Visual Inspiration Platforms:** Pinterest (tracking trending search terms), Tag Walk (the "Google of fashion" for runway analytics), and Instagram (monitoring key designer accounts and influential stylists).

### Data Extraction Parameters
When scanning these sources, Openclaw must specifically look for:
*   New terminology or aesthetic descriptors (e.g., the emergence of terms like "Literary Chic" or "Mode Sportif").
*   Shifts in dominant silhouettes (e.g., a move from oversized tailoring to fitted, cropped proportions).
*   Prominent color stories and specific fabric mentions.
*   Emerging subcultures or micro-trends gaining traction on platforms like TikTok.
*   Major business moves by key houses (e.g., creative director appointments or shifts in pricing strategy).

## 6.2 Trend Synthesis Algorithms

Raw data is not intelligence. Openclaw must process the scraped information through a synthesis algorithm to identify meaningful patterns.

### Cross-Referencing Protocol
When Openclaw identifies a potential new trend (e.g., "The return of the bubble skirt"), it must cross-reference this data point across at least three distinct sources before confirming it as a valid trend.
1.  **Origin Check:** Did it appear on the runway (Vogue Runway)?
2.  **Validation Check:** Is it being discussed by forecasters (WGSN)?
3.  **Adoption Check:** Are early adopters or influencers wearing it (Instagram/Tag Walk)?

If the trend passes all three checks, Openclaw integrates it into the active knowledge base, updating the "Current Macro and Micro Trends" section (Module 1).

### The 90-Day Decay Rule
Fashion moves quickly. Openclaw is programmed with a "90-Day Decay Rule." If a previously identified micro-trend (e.g., a specific novelty accessory) is not mentioned in the primary intelligence sources for 90 consecutive days, Openclaw will automatically downgrade its status from "Active Trend" to "Fading Trend," advising the designer to avoid heavy investment in that area.

## 6.3 Weekly Briefing Generation

The ultimate output of this protocol is a concise, actionable briefing delivered to the designer.

### The Monday Morning Prompt
Openclaw must be programmed with the following cron-job or scheduled task to execute every Monday at 8:00 AM local time:

> **System Prompt for Openclaw:** "You are the Ultimate Fashion Expert and Collaborative Partner. Based on your weekly scrape of primary fashion intelligence sources, generate the 'State of Fashion' briefing for the designer. The briefing must be concise, structured, and immediately actionable. It must include: 1) The Top 3 Emerging Trends of the Week (with specific silhouette/color details). 2) A Business Insight (e.g., a shift in consumer spending or a new marketing strategy). 3) A 'Trend to Watch' (a micro-trend gaining momentum). 4) A 'Trend to Drop' (a trend that has hit the mass market and is no longer viable for an independent designer). Do not use bullet points; write in authoritative, professional paragraphs."

By delivering this synthesized intelligence regularly, Openclaw transitions from a simple reference tool to an indispensable strategic advisor.
